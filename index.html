About
01

I find structure
where others find noise.

Design has never been something I do. It is how I think. I studied Strategic Design at Instituto Tecnológico y de Estudios Superiores de Occidente (ITESO), graduated Summa Cum Laude, then kept building: a Master's in Education (Summa Cum Laude), a Master's in UX Design, NNG's UX Management certification, and UI/UX for AI Products at Stanford. Before any of that, I spent 8 years studying haute couture — which taught me something no design school ever could: that making something beautiful and functional at the same time is never an accident.

For the past 6 years I have been a Senior Product Designer at Amazon Web Services, building products used across 100+ countries. Before AWS, I worked across Healthcare, Oil & Gas, Logistics, and Education. Each industry brought a different problem and a different set of constraints — that range is not a gap in focus. It is where the ability to walk into any room, read what is needed, and get to work comes from. Teaching was never separate from that. I spent 10 years as a professor at ITESO and Tec de Monterrey, teaching Strategic Design, UX, Editorial Design, and Digital Illustration. I have spoken at conferences in Mexico and the US, coached at Amazon's Creative Jam with Adobe, and served as jury at the Clap Awards and Veredictas FOROALFA. I mentor designers on ADPList and through the Amazon Mentoring Program.

I grew up in Mexico City and attended a German school. Two cultures, one work ethic: show up, do the work, be honest, and do not stop until it is right. I have a strong bias for action — if I do not know how to do something, I figure it out. Today Figma and AI are my closest collaborators, and I use them to go from ambiguity to functional prototype faster than most teams finish their first meeting. The tool will keep changing. The commitment to the user never will.

Process
02

From ambiguity
to clarity.

I do not wait for perfect information. I form a hypothesis, move fast, test it with real users, and adjust. Every stage is a chance to learn something that makes the next decision sharper. The brief can be vague. The deadline can be tight. Neither is a reason to skip the thinking.

01
Understand
I begin by understanding the problem as clearly as possible, no matter how it arrives. Sometimes it is a PRD. Sometimes it is a list of requirements, a spreadsheet, or just an early idea. I ask questions, look for what is missing, and define the core problem before moving into design. When I cannot speak with users directly, I create realistic user types and scenarios to guide decisions.
02
Frame
Once the problem is clearer, I shape it through the lens of usability, accessibility, and context. I think about clarity at first glance, strong hierarchy, keyboard navigation, language needs, and how the experience will work for different people in different situations. My goal is to make complex things feel simple without losing what matters.
03
Explore
From there, I start exploring. Ideas often begin as quick notes, rough sketches, or small fragments that help me think. I use feedback early and often with designers and product partners because good ideas get stronger when they are challenged. I do not get attached to a solution. I keep refining until the design serves the user in the best possible way.
04
Prototype & Validate
I prototype to understand the full experience and find where it may break. Prototyping helps me see the flow, the feedback states, and the small moments that shape the overall experience. Then I step back and evaluate through heuristics, expert review, and user testing. I also look for ways to reduce development effort, but never by compromising the user experience.
